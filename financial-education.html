Key Topics

Things Everyone Should Know

Financial literacy changes lives. Here are the fundamentals that can transform how you think about money and debt.

Fundamentals

What Is Debt — Really?

Debt is money you owe to lenders, whether through credit cards, personal loans, medical bills, or other obligations. But not all debt is the same. Understanding the difference between "good debt" and "bad debt" is the foundation of financial literacy. High-interest consumer debt is the silent wealth killer that keeps millions trapped.

Strategy

How to Actually Reduce Your Debt

Start by understanding exactly what you owe and at what interest rates. Then prioritize: attack the highest-interest debt first (avalanche method) or knock out the smallest balances for momentum (snowball method). Negotiate lower rates, consolidate where it makes sense, and always pay more than the minimum when you can.

Credit

How Debt Affects Your Credit Score

Unpaid or high-balance debt can significantly lower your credit score, affecting your ability to get loans, housing, and even jobs. But here's the good news: as you manage payments and reduce balances, your score can recover and even exceed where it was before. It's never too late to start rebuilding.

Budgeting

The Budget That Actually Works

Forget complicated spreadsheets. The best budget is the one you'll actually use. Start with the 50/30/20 rule: 50% for needs, 30% for wants, 20% for savings and debt payoff. Track your spending for just one month and you'll be shocked at where the money goes — and where you can find extra to put toward freedom.

Your Rights

Is Debt Relief Legal? Know Your Rights

Yes, debt relief programs are legal and follow specific guidelines designed to protect consumers. The Fair Debt Collection Practices Act (FDCPA) gives you rights against harassment. Understanding these protections is crucial — you have more power than you think when dealing with creditors and collectors.

Mindset

The Psychology of Debt

Debt is as much emotional as it is financial. The shame, anxiety, and avoidance that come with debt create a cycle that keeps people stuck. Breaking free starts with confronting the numbers, forgiving yourself, and taking that first small step. You didn't get into debt overnight, and you won't get out overnight — but you CAN get out.
